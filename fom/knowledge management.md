## 1. Understanding Data, Information, and Knowledge

### The Progression: Data to Information
**Original Text:** Data are facts, observations, or measures that have been recorded but not put into meaningful context .

### The Five Cs: Transforming Data into Information
To turn raw data into useful information, it must go through these five processes:
* **Condensed:** Data is summarized and unnecessary parts are removed.
* **Contextualized:** We understand why the data was collected.
* **Calculated:** Data is tabulated and analyzed.
* **Categorized:** The unit of analysis is known.
* **Corrected:** Errors are removed and missing "holes" are filled.

---

### Defining Knowledge
**Original Text:** Knowledge is a mix of framed experience, values, contextual information, expert insight and intuition that provides an environment and framework for evaluating and incorporating new experiences and information .
**Simple Explanation:** Knowledge isn't just facts; it's the ability to use information, experience, and gut feeling to make decisions or solve problems.

## 2. Knowledge Management (KM) Defined

### Core Definitions
**Original Text:** Knowledge Management is the creation, distribution and exploitation of knowledge to create and retain greater value of core business competencies .
**Simple Explanation:** KM is the practice of capturing the "collective brainpower" of an organization and making sure it's available to the right person at the right time to get the job done better.

### The Three Pillars (People, Process, Technology)
* **People:** The most important part. It involves creating a culture where people *want* to share what they know instead of hiding it.
* **Processes:** How the organization is structured to allow knowledge to flow.
* **Technology:** A tool that helps connect people, but it is not the solution by itself.

---

## 3. Types and Categories of Knowledge

### Explicit vs. Tacit Knowledge
* **Explicit Knowledge:** Knowledge that can be written down, codified, and stored in databases or manuals (e.g., a "how-to" guide) .
* **Tacit Knowledge:** Personal, context-specific knowledge stored in people's heads. It includes intuition, experience, and "tricks of the trade" that are hard to write down .

### Building Blocks of Knowledge
* **Externalization:** Converting tacit knowledge into explicit knowledge (e.g., writing a report after a project).
* **Socialization:** Sharing tacit knowledge through face-to-face interaction and experience.
* **Internalization:** Learning and absorbing explicit knowledge into one's own mental models.
* **Combination:** Combining different pieces of explicit knowledge to create new knowledge.

## 4. The Knowledge Economy

**Original Text:** The new source of wealth is knowledge, and not labor, land, or financial capital .
**Simple Explanation:** In the modern world, "brainpower" is more valuable than "machine power." Wealth comes from scientific discovery, software development, and specialized services rather than just farming or manufacturing.

## 5. KM Toolbox: Common Techniques

Organizations use several tools to manage knowledge:
1.  **After Action Reviews (AAR):** A quick meeting held immediately after an event to see what happened, why it happened, and how it can be done better next time.
2.  **Communities of Practice:** Groups of people who share a passion for something they do and learn how to do it better as they interact regularly.
3.  **Exit Interviews:** Capturing the knowledge of an employee before they leave so the company doesn't lose their expertise.
4.  **Peer Assists:** Asking colleagues for help and feedback before starting a new project.
5.  **Storytelling:** Using stories to share complex knowledge in a way that is easy to remember.
6.  **White Pages:** An advanced staff directory that lists "who knows what" so you can find experts quickly.

## 6. Barriers to Implementation

Implementing KM isn't always easy. Major obstacles include:
* **Cultural Resistance (48%):** The biggest barrier; people may be reluctant to share or change their habits.
* **Immaturity of Technology (19%):** Systems not being ready or user-friendly.
* **Cost (12%):** Lack of funding for KM initiatives.
* **Lack of Need (5%):** People not seeing the value in managing knowledge.
