# Marketing Management Notes: Unit 4

## 1. Definition of Marketing
**Original Text:** The management process through which goods and services move from concept to the customer. It includes the coordination of four elements called the 4 P's of marketing: Identification,
- selection and development of a product
- Determination of its price
- Selection of a distribution channel to reach the customer's place
- Development and implementation of a promotional strategy.

**Simple Explanation:** Marketing is the whole journey a product takes from being just an idea to ending up in a customer's hands. It involves deciding what to make, how much to charge, where to sell it, and how to tell people about it.

---

## 2. Marketing vs. Selling
**Original Text:** Marketing differs from selling because selling concerns itself with the tricks and techniques of getting people to exchange their cash for your product. Marketing invariably views the entire business process as consisting of a tightly integrated effort to discover, create, arouse and satisfy customer needs.

**Simple Explanation:** Selling is just about getting the money for the product you already have. Marketing is about figuring out what people actually need and then creating a product that satisfies them so they *want* to buy it.

## 3. The Seven Functions of Marketing
**Original Text:**
1. **Marketing Information Management:** Managing marketing information helps you understand your customers' needs.
2. **Distribution:** Determines how and where customers can obtain your products.
3. **Product/Service Management:** Identifying features to incorporate in new products and product upgrades.
4. **Pricing:** Determining market success and profitability; involves competition and cost considerations.
5. **Promotion:** Makes customers and prospects aware of your products and your company.
6. **Selling:** Creating awareness and building preference to help sales staff sell more.
7. **Financing:** Providing revenue for operations and offering customers payment methods like loans or credit.

**Simple Explanation:** These are the seven "jobs" marketing does: gathering data, moving the product, designing the product, setting the price, advertising, helping the sale happen, and managing the money/payment options.

---

## 4. The Marketing Mix (4 P's)
**Original Text:** The Marketing mix is a set of four decisions which needs to be taken before launching any new product:
1. **Product:** What the company is manufacturing?
2. **Price:** What is the pricing strategy used by the company?
3. **Place:** Where is the company selling?
4. **Promotions:** How is the company promoting the product?

**Simple Explanation:** The "Marketing Mix" is the recipe for success. You need the right product, at the right price, in the right place, with the right ads.

## 5. Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)
**Original Text:** A metric that represents the total net profit a company makes from any given customer. It is a projection to estimate a customer's monetary worth to a business after factoring in the value of the relationship with a customer over time.

**Simple Explanation:** CLV is a guess of how much total money a customer will spend with your business over their entire life. It helps companies decide how much they should spend to "win" a new customer.

---

## 6. New Product Development Stages
**Original Text:**
1. **Idea Generation:** Systematic search for new product ideas.
2. **Idea Screening:** Paring down ideas to those worth pursuing.
3. **Concept Development and Testing:** Developing a detailed version of the idea and testing it with consumers.
4. **Marketing Strategy Development:** Describing the target market and planned positioning.
5. **Business Analysis:** Reviewing projected sales, costs, and profits.
6. **Product Development:** Developing the concept into a physical prototype.
7. **Test Marketing:** Introducing the product to a realistic market setting.
8. **Commercialization:** Introducing the product to the full market.

**Simple Explanation:** This is the step-by-step process of making something new, from brainstorming to checking if it's profitable, making a prototype, and finally selling it to everyone.

---

## 7. Unethical Issues in Marketing
**Original Text:** Unethical marketing can send wrong signals about your products, destroy your brand's reputation, and possibly lead to legal problems. Examples include:
* Making false or exaggerated claims.
* Distortion of facts to mislead buyers.
* Concealing side effects of products.
* Bad-mouthing rival products.
* Using fear tactics (e.g., "buy now or the price goes up").
* Spamming (unsolicited emails).

**Simple Explanation:** This is "bad" marketing—lying about what a product does, hiding the dangers, or pressuring people into buying things through fear or annoying emails.
