# Financial Management: Unit 3 Study Guide

## 1. Introduction to Financial Management
Financial management refers to the managerial activities involved in the planning and controlling of a firm's financial resources .

## 2. Scope of Financial Management
The scope and functions of financial management are generally classified into two approaches :

### A. Traditional Approach
* **Focus**: This approach restricts the finance function to the "procurement of funds" to meet financial needs .
* **Procurement**: Refers to raising funds externally and managing the related institutional, legal, and accounting arrangements between the firm and its sources .
* **Limitations**:
    * It only considers the procurement of funds and ignores the **allocation** of funds .
    * It focuses on outside parties (investors, bankers) rather than internal decision-making .
    * It neglects working capital management and overall financial decision-making .

### B. Modern Approach
* **Focus**: An analytical approach that covers both the **acquisition** and the **allocation** of funds .
* **Key Questions Answered**:
    * How large should the enterprise be and how fast should it grow? 
    * In what form should the firm hold its assets? 
    * How should the required funds be raised?

## 3. Core Functions of Finance
There are three primary finance functions :

### I. Investment Decision
Relates to the selection of assets in which funds will be invested .
* **Capital Budgeting**: Decisions regarding **long-term assets** that yield benefits in the future .
* **Working Capital Management**: Decisions regarding **short-term or current assets** . 
    * **Goal**: Ensure liquidity and solvency .
    * **The Conflict**: There is a trade-off between **profitability** and **liquidity**. Too little investment leads to illiquidity; too much reduces profitability .

### II. Financing Decision
Concerned with the **financing mix** or **capital structure** .
* **Capital Structure**: The specific mix of debt and equity used by a firm .
* **Objective**: Determine the best combination and then raise the appropriate amount through available sources .

![[Pasted image 20260509014801.png]]
### III. Dividend Decision
Relates to whether a firm should distribute profits to shareholders or retain them for reinvestment .
* **Optimum Policy**: The policy that maximizes the value of shares and shareholder wealth .
* **Payout Ratio**: The proportion of net profit paid out to shareholders .

## 4. Objectives of Financial Management
1.  **Profit Maximization**: Based on economic theory, this objective suggests firms should maximize profits to ensure efficient resource utilization and social welfare .
2.  **Wealth Maximization**: Also known as Net Present Value (NPV) maximisation. It is considered a more appropriate and operationally feasible criterion for financial decisions .

## 5. Sources of Finance
Capital requirements are split into two categories :
* **Fixed Capital**: Used for long-term production facilities like plant, machinery, land, and buildings .
* **Working Capital**: Used for short-term needs like raw materials, wages, and day-to-day expenses .

### Classification by Term
| Term | Sources |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Short-Term** | Bank Credit, Customer Advances, Trade Credit  |
| **Medium-Term** | Debentures, Preference Shares, Bank Loans, Public Deposits  |
| **Long-Term** | Issue of Equity Shares, Debentures, Retained Earnings (Ploughing back of profits), Loans from financial institutions  |

## 6. Types of Ownership Securities (Shares)
Shares represent the "owned capital" of a company .

### Equity Shares
* **Residual Claim**: Equity holders are paid dividends after preference shareholders .
* **Control**: Equity shareholders have voting rights and control the company .
* **Risk/Reward**: High dividends during prosperity, but risk of zero returns during adversity .

### Preference Shares
* **Preferential Rights**: They have priority over equity shares for dividend payments and repayment of capital during liquidation .
* **Fixed Return**: The dividend rate is fixed .
